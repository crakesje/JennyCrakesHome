Module 11: Workshop Report

Working in the Scrum team was particularly useful because it provided a simultaneous opportunity for us to receive feedback and ask questions about our own work, to help others with their work, and to learn from and be inspired by others’ design choices. Overall, my group was very helpful to me as I addressed different obstacles in the design process.

On our first Scrum day, I showed my team members my sitemap and navigation scheme. They liked the idea of having my resume on its own page, and said it would be good to display the resume text as well as having a downloadable .pdf, a suggestion I followed in making the actual website page. They also said it might be a good idea to have thumbnail images that would link to full-size ones.

I showed by Scrum team members my design comps on our second scrum day, and they provided feedback to me to help me progress. On the landing page, they advised shrinking the photo a little, going with an entirely blue background rather than the pink text box, and re-using the royal blue of the header for body text. On my About page, they linked the diagonal layout of photographs in opposite corners. I had initially planned to have each project in my portfolio link to its own page, but they told me that may be too much work, and advised starting out small. They said that three work examples on each portfolio subpage seemed like a good number to them. They also gave me advice regarding technical processes, such as how to connect Codepen to Github, and how to handle subfolders within the main WRA 210 folder.

On our Scrum days, I provided advice to my team members regarding the aesthetics of their design choices, especially color choice, font, and arrangement; and gave comments regarding the professionalism of their pages and helping correct typos or language errors. Because I tended to be behind the rest of the group, I was less useful in terms of technical questions, but was able to provide feedback on the appearance of their pages and the content they chose to include. I believe this was helpful to them when they were uncertain.

Because I had not begun developing my actual website at the time of my portfolio presentation (I just had the design comps), I received significant feedback during the presentation that I could apply in building my portfolio website. Some feedback I received regarding color was to get rid of the stark white I had used to make text boxes on my Photoshop design comps. I was advised to use more of my purple instead of white, to help tie things together and not complicate the page with too many colors. I followed this advice on my CodePen pages, sticking with the light blue background, and purple and royal blue text. I was also advised to avoid too many font variations, and to make sure I chose websafe fonts. I ended up choosing to use just two fonts: Lucida Calligraphy and Helvetica. Professor Daniels also said I may not need an internal search bar for my site, and could use Google Forms to create a contact form.

I primarily got support from Professor Daniels and from my scrum team members. Asking questions in-person was a good way to make sure I understood detailed processes, and an opportunity to watch others perform complicated steps. Other resources I used were exercises on Codecademy’s HTML and CSS track, information from the Prezis presented to our class and housed on our course website, Karl Stolley’s book How to Design and Write Web Pages Today, and Google searches when I needed to remind myself of the answer to a specific question, such as how to center text. In making my design docs, I got some additional advice from consultants in the RCAH’s Language and Media Center, specifically how to use layers in Photoshop, which I hadn’t done before.
